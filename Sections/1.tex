\section{On the correct choice of models}
Polyhedra enjoy many nice visualization properties due to their 'small' nature:
The picture most people -among those, the author- have in mind when talking about Polyhedra
is a compact, connected subset of $\R^n$.

While this picture is amazing for reasoning and for applications, it gets in the way of the goal of this paper:

Recall 
\begin{theorem}[Goldblatt-Thomason]\hfill 

A class of (Kripke) frames is modally definable if and only if:
\begin{itemize}
    \item It's closed under bounded morphic images,
    \item It's closed under (arbitrary) disjoint unions,
    \item It's closed under - and reflects ultrafilter extensions.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

Images do nothing to subtract from our understanding of Polyhedra, but that is the only non-issue:

A class closed under arbitrary disjoint unions must contain a a non-compact non-connected space: 
Say we tried to generate a class from the polyhedron consisting of a single vertex,
 we can obtain an infinite discrete set of $\aleph_0$ vertices 
 by taking the disjoint union of the point with itself $\aleph_0$ times.

 An even bigger problem is the closure under ultrafilter extensions: 
 Say we have a polyhedron with infinite cardinality $\aleph_0$ -which we must, as was argued just above-,
its ultrafilter extension must have cardinality $2^{2^{\aleph_0}}$ %citation
, which is too big to be a subset of $\R^n$ for any finite $n$.

Annoyingly enough, we expect those extension to be well-behaved dimension-wise %citation 
(the ultrafilter extension an n-dimensional polyhedron should be n-dimensional too as they express the same logic)
meaning that embedding it in $\R^\kappa$ for an appropriate cardinal $\kappa$ wouldn't do us any favors, 
as we should always be able to find a finite-dimensional space (thus of smaller cardinality than our set) in which it's embeddable. %citation

So, what do we propose as models?

\subsection{Simplicial complexes}
Simplicial complexes are the most widely used model of polyhedra: They give all the topological information about them with minimal size.

\begin{definition}[Simplicial Complex]\hfill 

    A \textbf{Simplicial complex} is a pair $(V,\Sigma)$ such that $\Sigma\subseteq\Pp(V)$ is closed under taking subsets.

    We call the elements of $\Sigma$ \textbf{Simplices} and we say that $\tau$ is a \textbf{face} of $\sigma$ -denoted $\tau\prec\sigma$-
    whenever $\tau \subseteq \sigma$. 

    We will often avoid talking about $V$ and regard $\Sigma$ as the fundamental object, since $V$ can be extracted as its singletons.

    A \textbf{Map} of simplicial complexes $(V,\Sigma)\to(V\sigma')$ is a function $f:V\to V'$ such that the image of every simplex of $\Sigma$ is a simplex of $\Sigma'$.
\end{definition}

This is a pretty standard combinatorial description, that has its geometric counterpart:

\begin{definition}[Geometric realization]\hfill 

    Consider a simplicial complex $\Sigma$ regarded as a preorder with its face relation. Construct a functor $G:\Sigma\to\Top$ as follows:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $G(\sigma)=\Delta^{|\sigma|}$ (the simplex of order "cardinality of $\sigma$")
        \item $G(\tau\leq\sigma):\Delta^m\to\Delta^n$ is the embedding of one of its faces.
    \end{itemize}

    In this, we modify the existing structure a little:
    We call $\tolde{G}$ the functor $\tolde{G}(X)=I(G(X))$ where $I$ denotes the \textbf{Relative interior}, that is, the interior of the face seen as immersed 
    in the smallest possible real affine space contaning it.
    as it's usually the case.

    The \textbf{Geometric realization} of $\Sigma$ is the colimit $|\Sigma|:=\lim\limits_{\to} \tolde{G}$.

    In other words it's the quotient space $\displaystyle{\frac{\coprod_{\sigma\in\Sigma}(G(\sigma))^o}{\sim}}$ where $\sim$ identifies each point
     with its face embedding.
\end{definition}

Let us define: 
\begin{definition}[Polyhedra]\hfill 

    Let $\Sigma,\Sigma'$ be two simplicial complexes. We say that $\Sigma\sim_p \Sigma'$ (read: $\Sigma$ and $\Sigma'$ are \textbf{Polyhedrally Equivalent}) 
    whenever they have the same realization \footnote{Here 'the same' is a bit of a vague concept: Asking for \textbf{equality} is -as usual- too restrictive
    but \textbf{homeomorphism} is a bit too loose (recall that a square and a triangle are very much homeomorphic. We will discuss what the appropriate notion of 
    "sameness" is in a later section)}
     %MORE PRECISE

    A \textbf{Polyhedron} $P$ is an equivalence class of simplicial complexes through $\sim_p$.
\end{definition}

We have a way to pass from simplicial complexes to topological spaces, now we need a way to go back.

We need a couple of checks:

\begin{proposition}[Equivalence and triangulation]
    Let $\Sigma$ be a simplicial complex and $x\in |\Sigma|$ any point on its realization.
    
    Then there exists a complex $\Sigma'$ such that $\Sigma'\sim_p\Sigma$ and $x$ is the realization of a vertex of $\Sigma'$
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}\hfill 

    Consider the simplicial complex $P$ made of the vertices of $|\Sigma|$ (in $\Top$).
    This realizes to itself, thus being equivalent to $|\Sigma|$. 

    Now consider the unique face $\sigma=(y_1,...,y_n)$ whose relative interior contains $x$.
    Add the lines $(xy_i)_{i=1,...,n}$, then the triangles $(xy_iy_j)_{i<j=1,...,n}$, etcetera \\
    until the n-simplices $(xy_{i_1}\cdots y_{i_{n-1}})_{i_1<...<i_{n-1}=1,...,n}$

    As everything is contained in the same face $\sigma$ we obtain the same realization as $P$, thus the same realization as $\Sigma$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[$\sim_p$ induces morphisms]\hfill 

Let $\Sigma$ and $\Sigma'$ be two polyhedrally equivalent simplicial complexes, then there exists a simplicial complex $\Sigma''$ in the same equivalence class
and a pair of monomorphisms 
\[\begin{tikzcd}[ampersand replacement=\&]
	\Sigma \& {\Sigma''} \& {\Sigma'}
	\arrow[tail, from=1-1, to=1-2]
	\arrow[tail, from=1-3, to=1-2]
\end{tikzcd}\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}\hfill 

This is non other than the triangulation lemma:
We simply repeat the process described in the lemma above for all points not in common between the two.

The original faces are maintained thus the two maps are simply inclusions.
%\cite{}
\end{proof}
